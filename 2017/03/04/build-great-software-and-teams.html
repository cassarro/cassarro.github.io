Building Software and Teams

March 4, 2017| By Roderick Cassar Tags:

An Innovative and Collaborative Culture.

Software developers spend a lot of time and energy planning on how to build the best possible products. We get carried away over which web framework to use or whether to use a NoSQL or SQL database. Such questions are important, yet they often neglect to address an important aspect of software development - culture. A strong innovative and collaborative culture.

Business products constantly change and evolve. It can happen that in the next few years, a product that you’ve poured your blood, sweat, and tears into may not exist anymore. However, if you’ve also focused on creating a strong team and coding culture, you will always be poised to take advantage of opportunities (and overcome obstacles) that come your way. Furthermore, developing a strong culture requires a shared understanding of what your team stands for and this cohesion will be reflected positively in the products that your team builds.

Great culture contributes to great products.

So what defines a “great culture”?

Our office environment is ultimately not our working environment. We can have access to fitness classes, yoga, beer Fridays and more, but these do not equate to how we talk, work, organize, win, and lose together. This is what makes a great working culture. It is that which leads to happier, more productive employees. All the free food and office perks in the world are useless if people feel afraid of failure, trapped in a rigid hierarchy, or that their employer values profits over people.

Our workplace today is no longer dominated by a 9-to-5, clock-in clock-out, production-line assembly mentality. For knowledge workers, like software developers, output isn’t valued by volume or hours, but by creativity and quality. We have now the freedom to work in the office, at home or in a hotel room whenever we want. This creates infinitely exciting opportunities to cultivate a culture that benefits every employee. This obvisouly assumes employees are responsible and mature.

Developing a strong culture requires a shared understanding of what your team stands for and this cohesion will be reflected positively in the products that your team builds. A weight of research has emerged that proves something we already intuitively know:

happy employees are productive employees and generally enable more profitable, faster-growing companies.

Everyone should always be aware of the customer. Customer awareness also helps teams stay passionate about their products. To sustain team work, one must beware of prima donnas in the team, who may over shoot the goal or do things that aren’t good for the entire team or the product.

Team Size

We shoot for small teams with an average of four to seven people. However, as companies get bigger and bigger, these teams and departments tend to get divided into silos that can be difficult to breach. There are inter-departmental chat rooms in addition to chats for each team that allows people from different parts of the organization to connect. We know that our culture will change as we grow even larger, but we also know that our values will help keep our culture intact if we do the right thing and hold people accountable living them. We scale culture by creating small development teams that also include designers and QA people and that are cross-functional, so if we don’t need a designer for the next sprint, he or she can go to another team that needs them, and vice versa.

Another challenge is organizing multiple teams that work on one product. I’ve seen companies address this by creating a front-end team, performance team, database team, etc. I found that giving each team responsibility for a specific area works much better.

Picking the Right People is one of the greatest efforts to cultivate a culture so it will not fall short.

Thus, always hire people that are a good fit for your company and each team. Its better to wait longer to hire, than to hire people who won’t fit well within the team.
