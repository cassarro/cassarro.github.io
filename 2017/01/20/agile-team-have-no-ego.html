Agile Teams Have No Ego

January 20, 2017| By Roderick Cassar Tags:

Software Developers

Software developers are talented, smart, skilled professionals. We put in lots of our own effort to educate ourselves and stay up to speed on latest technologies. We work hard and we feel great satisfaction in our achievements. All things considered, there are a lot of really great programmers out there.

Agile software development with Scrum is often perceived as a methodology but rather than viewing Scrum as methodology, think it as a framework for managing a process. Scrum relies on a self-organizing, cross-functional team. Thus, the team supersedes the individual, yet keeping in mind that a number of individuals make up a team.

There is no ‘best’ individual

I lead a number of teams of software developers. Does that mean I am the “best” programmer within the team? Certainly not. It just means that I lead the teams. As the leader of the teams, it is my job to: inspire, encourage, trail blaze, and motivate my teams to be the best they can be. So is there a best programmer in a team? Most probably there is, but then its up to the individual’s ego and attitude to either help others become as good or become the prima donna of the team. There shouldn’t be a “best” programmer on the team. Everyone on the team is great and working hard. That said, hard work and efforts must be noticed and such feedback should be given.

That said, any team members who are under performing within a team, should have the humility to ask for help. It is all about the attitude. When someone shows the effort to improve, by for instance taking a course in his private time to get up to speed on a technology, would need the support of his or her team. If team members work as a team, pulling the same string towards the same direction, the results acheived are immense.

Then again, if one’s ego would be to bad mouth such an under performing individual other than giving a helping hand, then the team starts to get flaky. One must understand the big difference between constructive feedback and destructive feedback. Which ever type of feedback one chooses would show the ego one has or has not.

A team that really meshes and works well together doesn’t let egos get in the way. It doesn’t matter if you’re a rock star developer. When you’re on the team, you’re part of the team. You might be the JavaScript expert, the master of SQL queries, or the guy who can refactor anything. When you’re part of the team, you should try to use your strength, but don’t let that interfere with the team’s flow.

Coming together is a beginning; keeping together is progress; working together is success - Henry Ford

Pair Programming

Sometimes it makes sense for a more experience developer to work with a less experienced developer. A common first instinct is that the senior person is mentoring the junior person, but it’s actually going both ways. Each of the two developers is bringing a lot to the table. Their different experiences and views allow them to each approach the problems differently, ask different questions, and apply different, existing knowledge.

If either person’s ego gets in the way, it would prevent the 2-way knowledge transfer in addition to creating friction between them. One Developer and one Junior Developer can pair on a task and share knowledge. Does one act like the other one is just along for the ride? No.

Your pair buddy, is watching your back, guiding the team, and keeping the pair honest. Which one does that job? Both. If you had one person drive the whole time, you’re not going to see the benefits of pair programming. If you let your ego get in the way, you might not let the junior developer write any of the code. They may not know every library, every design pattern, or even the intricacies of the language they’re using, but those guys can write some great code.

How the pairs split up doesn’t really matter. Who is working with whom doesn’t really matter. Each and every member of the team is bringing something to the table, and it is this interaction that is making the team as effective as it is.

Team vs Individual

We can accomplish a lot of stuff when our team works individually, however, we can accomplish a lot more as a team. You might be the “best developer” on your team, but if you keep thinking and acting that way, you’ll be missing out on a lot. I learn a lot when I pair with any member of my team. It doesn’t matter how much experience the person has. Everyone knows something I don’t, and no one approaches the problems exactly the same way I would.

There is no “Ego” in “Agile Team”, so don’t bring yours to an agile team and expect good results.
